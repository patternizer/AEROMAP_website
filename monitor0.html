AEROMAP's global aerosol model calculates aerosol microphysical parameters on the daily timescale and over the whole Earth surface at a spatial resolution of 1x1 degree. It's neural networks convert daily satellite measurements of optical depth and columnar water vapour to aerosol products that include the volume size distribution and its derived parameters - the volume concentration of fine and coarse particles V(f) and V(c) as well as their geometric radii r(f) and r(c) in each pixel. From a multiparametric weighting of these parameters, AEROMAP also calculates 2 air quality indices (normalized to the international 1-10 scale): one as a measure of the hazard to human health and the second as a measure of the impact on visibility. On this page we present selected model outputs. Please contact us if you have a specific data request or would like to use our simulations in a research collaboration. Visit the Data Request and Research Collaboration page for further details.

Air quality indices

AEROMAP has constructed 2 aerosol quality indices (AQIs): AQI-1 (hazard to health) and AQI-2 (visibility impairment) from weighted combinations of the microphysical parameters (a manuscript will shortly be submitted outlining the methodology). The indices are normalized to the internationally-adopted scale 1-10 used for existing indices calculated from chemical compositions. On this scale, the following categories are often used: 1-2=Very low, 3-4=Low, 5-6=Moderate, 7-8=High and 9-10=Very High.

Monitoring the Karthala Eruption

In order to test the feasibility of monitoring the temporal variation and dispersion of AEROMAP-derived microphysical parameters, daily maps of V(f), V(c), r(f) and r(c) were generated for the southern part of the African continent for the period 11-22 January 2007 when the Karthala volcano on the island of Madagascar erupted.
